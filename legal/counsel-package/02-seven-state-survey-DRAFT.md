# 02 — Seven-State Survey DRAFT (unverified — every citation needs counsel confirmation)

**Status: AI-assisted research draft.** Citations were compiled from general
knowledge of skill-contest / DFS law patterns as of early 2026 and have NOT
been checked against current codes, session laws, or agency guidance. Each is
tagged **[VERIFY]**. Draft determinations are hypotheses, not conclusions.

**The product analyzed:** pay-per-round trivia (see `01-product-ground-truth.md`).
The recurring analytical frame in every state is: (a) does the state use a
*predominance/dominant-factor* test, a *material-element* test, or an *any-chance*
test for the chance element; (b) does an entry-fee-for-prize contest constitute
a "bet/wager" or unlawful lottery when skill predominates; (c) do any
contest-registration, bonding, or fantasy-contest licensing regimes reach this
product; (d) do any of this product's specific features (escalating per-round
fees, streak bonus, sudden-death tiebreaker, staff-varied payout schemes,
zero-winner refund / possible future rollover) change the answer.

Features counsel should weigh in EVERY state:
- **Escalating per-round micro-entries** — unlike DFS's single entry fee, a
  player's total consideration is variable and partly driven by in-game events.
- **Trivia as skill** — knowledge-based Q&A under time pressure has an older and
  cleaner skill pedigree than DFS (no reliance on third-party athletes'
  performance); but question selection/difficulty is operator-controlled, and a
  randomness argument can be made from question assignment. Server assigns the
  same question to all players in a round, which mitigates this.
- **Streak bonus (free round)** — a prize-linked mechanic inside the contest.
- **Payout-scheme variability** — disclosed pre-entry, but varies per game.
- **Rake (40%)** — operator takes a percentage; in some states "percentage
  game" language matters [VERIFY per state].

## California — draft: PERMIT (with consumer-protection compliance)

- Lottery: Cal. Penal Code § 319 (prize + chance + consideration) [VERIFY].
  Gambling/banking or percentage games: Penal Code § 330 [VERIFY].
- Test: dominant-factor / predominance per case law (e.g., *Finster v. Keller*,
  18 Cal. App. 3d 836 (1971)) [VERIFY current leading authority].
- Skill contests with entry fees and prizes are the long-standing basis for the
  skill-gaming industry operating in CA; trivia should sit comfortably on the
  skill side under a dominant-factor test.
- **Wrinkle:** Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17539–17539.6 (contest disclosure
  statutes) may impose disclosure/operational duties on skill contests where
  payment is required [VERIFY applicability to this format]. Payout-scheme
  variability (§3.10) should be analyzed under these disclosure rules.
- No known registration/bonding for skill contests [VERIFY].

## Texas — draft: PERMIT (bona fide skill-contest exception), flag AG posture

- Tex. Penal Code § 47.02 (gambling); § 47.01(1) defines "bet" to include
  outcomes determined "partially by chance" — an unusually broad chance test —
  BUT § 47.01(1)(B) excludes "an offer of a prize, award, or compensation to
  the actual contestants in a bona fide contest for the determination of
  skill..." [VERIFY text and construction].
- Analysis: players are actual contestants; trivia is a determination of skill;
  the exception should apply notwithstanding the "partially by chance" bet
  definition. The 40% rake and escalating fees need analysis under the
  "actual contestants" and bona-fides case law [VERIFY].
- **Flag:** Tex. Att'y Gen. Op. KP-0057 (2016) treated paid DFS as likely
  illegal gambling [VERIFY] — distinguishable (DFS outcomes ride on third-party
  athletes; trivia outcomes are the contestant's own performance), but it
  signals AG hostility to paid-entry contest products generally.

## Ohio — draft: PERMIT, but clear the "scheme of chance" and SBAM definitions

- Ohio Rev. Code Ch. 2915: gambling offenses; § 2915.01 defines "scheme of
  chance" and "game of chance" [VERIFY definitions and whether a
  skill-predominant paid contest falls outside them].
- **Wrinkle 1:** Ohio's skill-based amusement machine (SBAM) regime
  (§ 2915.01, merchandise-prize caps) heavily restricts "skill-based amusement
  machines" [VERIFY]. Counsel should confirm a consumer's own phone running
  this app cannot be swept into the SBAM definitions — the definitions are
  device-oriented and this is a distribution question worth an explicit answer.
- **Wrinkle 2:** Ohio licenses fantasy contests (Ohio Rev. Code Ch. 3774,
  Casino Control Commission) [VERIFY]. Definition appears tied to sports
  statistics; confirm trivia falls outside Ch. 3774 rather than inside an
  unlicensed version of it.

## Massachusetts — draft: PERMIT, with attention to AG consumer-protection posture

- Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 271 (gaming, lotteries) [VERIFY sections]; dominant-factor

- **Wrinkle:** the MA Attorney General's DFS regulations, 940 CMR 34.00 (2016)

## New Jersey — draft: LEAN PERMIT, but confirm regulator posture explicitly

## Cross-cutting federal overlay (applies to all seven)

- **UIGEA**, 31 U.S.C. §§ 5361–5367 [VERIFY]: turns on whether the underlying
  bet/wager is unlawful under the law of the state where made — a state-law
  question, plus the § 5362(1) definitions. If skill-predominant and lawful in
  the states served, UIGEA should not be triggered; counsel to confirm.
- **Illegal Gambling Business Act**, 18 U.S.C. § 1955 [VERIFY]: predicated on a
  state-law violation; same dependency.
- **Wire Act**, 18 U.S.C. § 1084: limited to sports betting per *NHLC v. DOJ*
  (1st Cir. 2021) [VERIFY]; should not apply.
- **Money transmission / FinCEN**: whether holding player wallet balances makes
  the operator a money transmitter (31 C.F.R. § 1010.100(ff) [VERIFY] and
  state MTL statutes in each of the seven) — see question 3.1 in document 03.
